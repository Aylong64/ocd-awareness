This is an awareness and help website for people living with Obsessive-Compulsive-Disorder(OCD). This website was built to reach out to people living in third world countries where they might not be an awareness of this disorder. This site contains information on what OCD is, symptoms, treatments(Conventional and Unconventional techniques) and Resources to help you. Being diagnosed with OCD is not a death notification.

ABOUT OCD

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a mental health disorder that causes people to have unwanted and intrusive thoughts (obsessions) and to feel the need to perform repetitive behaviors (compulsions) in response to those thoughts.

OCD can be very debilitating, but it is treatable. There are a variety of medications and therapies that can help people with OCD manage their symptoms and live normal lives.

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a mental health disorder characterized by unwanted and intrusive thoughts (obsessions) and repetitive behaviors (compulsions). These obsessions and compulsions are time-consuming and can interfere with daily life. Obsessions are unwanted and intrusive thoughts, images, or urges that cause anxiety. People with OCD may try to ignore or suppress their obsessions, but they often find this difficult. Some common obsessions include:

  • Fear of contamination
  • Doubts about whether something has been done correctly
  • Need for symmetry or order
  • Harmful or violent thoughts
  • Sexual thoughts
Compulsions are repetitive behaviors or mental acts that a person with OCD feels driven to do in response to their obsessions. Compulsions are often performed in an attempt to reduce anxiety or distress caused by the obsessions. Some common compulsions include:
  • Washing hands repeatedly
  • Checking locks or appliances repeatedly
  • Counting things repeatedly
  • Arranging objects in a specific way
  • words or phrases

TREATMENT

  1. Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)

    2. This is a type of therapy that helps people to change the way they think and behave. In CBT for OCD, people learn to identify and challenge their obsessive thoughts and to resist the urge to perform compulsions. CBT is often combined with exposure therapy, which involves gradually exposing people to their feared triggers without allowing them to perform their compulsions.

  2. Medication

    3. Medication can also be an effective treatment for OCD. The most commonly prescribed medications for OCD are selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), which work by increasing the levels of serotonin in the brain. SSRIs can take several weeks or months to start working, and they may not be effective for everyone. In some cases, people with OCD may need to combine CBT and medication to get the best results. Treatment for OCD is usually long-term, but most people with OCD can learn to manage their symptoms and live full and productive lives.

  3. Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT)

    4. This is a type of therapy that teaches people to accept their thoughts and feelings without judgment. ACT can be helpful for people with OCD who find it difficult to control their thoughts and urges.

  4. Mindfulness-based therapy (MBT)

    5. This is a type of therapy that teaches people to focus on the present moment and to accept their thoughts and feelings without judgment. MBT can be helpful for people with OCD who find it difficult to relax and let go of their obsessions and compulsions.

  5. Eye movement desensitization and reprocessing (EMDR)

    6. This is a type of therapy that uses eye movements to help people process traumatic memories. EMDR can be helpful for people with OCD who have experienced trauma.

Self-Help Tips

  1. Relaxation techniques. Relaxation techniques such as deep breathing, meditation, and progressive muscle relaxation can help to reduce anxiety and stress, which can trigger OCD symptoms.
  2. Get enough sleep. When you're well-rested, you're better able to cope with stress and anxiety. Aim for 7-8 hours of sleep per night.
  3. Eat a healthy diet. Eating a healthy diet can help to improve your overall mood and well-being. Make sure to include plenty of fruits, vegetables, and whole grains in your diet.
  4. Exercise regularly. Exercise is a great way to reduce stress and anxiety, and it can also improve your mood and self-esteem. Aim for at least 30 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise most days of the week.
  5. Avoid caffeine and alcohol. Caffeine and alcohol can worsen OCD symptoms. If you find that these substances trigger your symptoms, it's best to avoid them.
  6. Talk to someone you trust. Talking to a friend, family member, therapist, or other trusted person can help you to feel less alone and more supported. They can also offer you guidance and support as you manage your OCD.

Note: These self-help strategies are not a substitute for professional treatment. If you are struggling with OCD, it's important to seek professional help from a therapist or psychiatrist. With the right treatment, you can manage your OCD and live a happy and fulfilling life.
